Every morning, before anything else, I spend two hours writing and revising notes. Not answering emails. Not reading Twitter. Not planning my day. Just thinking in writing: developing ideas, making connections, sharpening claims.

This is the most important habit I have built. It is also the hardest to maintain. But nothing else in my workflow produces as much return per minute invested.

## Why Morning

Mornings are the only time my mind is genuinely clear. There are no accumulated distractions from the day. No urgent requests. No mental residue from conversations and decisions. Just a blank space and whatever I wrote yesterday.

Writing is the hardest cognitive work I do. It requires holding multiple ideas in mind simultaneously, evaluating connections, finding precise words for imprecise thoughts. I want to do it when I have the most cognitive resources available.

By afternoon, my capacity for this kind of work is diminished. I can still read. I can still answer emails. I can still have conversations. But the deep, focused writing that produces my best thinking only happens in those first two hours.

## What I Actually Do

The morning session has a simple structure:

First, I review what I wrote yesterday. This reconnects me to where I left off and often sparks new ideas about old topics. Many of my best revisions happen in these first ten minutes, when I see yesterday's writing with fresh eyes.

Then, I process my writing inbox. Throughout the previous day, I collected fragments: an interesting quote, a half-formed idea, a question that occurred to me while walking. Each fragment gets a few minutes of attention. Some become full notes. Some get discarded. Some get combined. The inbox is a garden, and every morning I tend it.

Finally, I write new notes or revise old ones. I follow whatever seems most interesting or urgent. I try not to plan this part too rigidly. The best insights emerge when I let my attention roam across my notes, following links, discovering connections I had not planned to make.

## The Hardest Part

The hardest part is starting. Every morning, there is a moment when my brain offers perfectly reasonable excuses: check email first, read one article, just finish that small task from yesterday.

I have learned to recognize this as resistance, not reason. The cure is mechanical: sit down, open the notes, start reading. Within two minutes, the resistance dissolves. The ideas take over. The thinking begins.

This is why the routine matters more than the output. Some mornings produce brilliant insights. Some produce nothing usable. The routine does not care. It just asks me to show up and think.
