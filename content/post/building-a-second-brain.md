Most note-taking advice is about storage: how to save more, organize better, find faster. But the point of notes is not storage. The point is thinking.

## Notes Are Not for Remembering

A note system that only stores information is just a fancy filing cabinet. You put things in. Years later, you might find them again. But the notes themselves are dead. They sit unchanged while you grow, learn, and evolve past them.

The alternative is living notes. Notes that you revise every time you encounter them. Notes that link to each other so densely that reading one pulls you into a web of connected ideas. Notes that represent your current best understanding, not a frozen snapshot of what you once thought.

My notes think. They evolve. Every time I open an old note, I revise it. Every time I learn something new, I connect it. Over months and years, my notes compound: each one a brick in a structure that gets stronger, not dustier.

## One Note, One Idea

The most important rule: each note captures exactly one idea. Not a topic. Not a chapter summary. One specific, well-defined claim.

This constraint forces clarity. If you cannot state an idea in a single note, you do not understand it well enough. If a note covers three ideas, split it into three. If a note covers none, delete it.

The title is the claim. The body is the evidence. The links are the context.

## Organized by Concept, Not by Source

Most people organize notes by where they came from: lecture notes, book notes, meeting notes. This makes sense when you are capturing. It makes no sense when you are thinking.

Ideas do not care which book they came from. A concept from a physics textbook connects to a concept from a philosophy paper connects to a concept from a conversation last week. If your notes are separated by source, those connections are invisible.

Organize by concept. Every note about "memory consolidation" lives together in the network, regardless of whether the insight came from a neuroscience paper, a personal experience, or a YouTube video.

## Dense Linking Is Not Optional

A note with no links is an island. You might visit it once and never return. A note with ten links is a hub. Every path through your notes passes through it. You encounter it from multiple angles, in multiple contexts, and each time you add something new.

Linking is not a finishing touch. It is the primary mechanism by which notes become useful. When you write a new note, search for related existing notes. Link to them. Link from them. If a connection seems surprising, that is a sign you should write about it.

## Written for Your Future Self

Your audience is not the internet. Your audience is you, six months from now, trying to remember what you were thinking.

Write with enough context that your future self can understand without rereading the source material. Write with enough precision that your future self can build on the idea without first having to reconstruct it. Write with enough honesty that your future self can see where you were wrong and improve.
